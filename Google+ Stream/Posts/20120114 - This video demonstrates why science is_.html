Further thoughts:

I'm not an economist (nor accountant, nor politician, nor statistician), but I got to wondering if there's a correlation between the 2011 Science and Engineering Readiness Index (SERI) scores per state: http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/07/11/state-education-rankings-_n_894528.html

... and the amount of money each state spent on education (per capita) the previous year: http://www.usgovernmentspending.com/compare_state_spending_2010h20a

The northeast clearly lines up, but there are many outliers. I collected the data into a spreadsheet to look at the ratio of SERI to spending, which can be thought of as an educational efficiency score (smartness per doller): https://docs.google.com/spreadsheet/ccc?key=0ApfbKyZpGyP5dFd0WVFlUjQxTUNDUXZ3TTZTQmRnVnc

I found no clear correlation between SERI and spending, but I did notice one thing: California is dead last in my "educational efficiency" score, with a lot of dollars spent for a pretty weak SERI rating (note that these dollars are per-capita; it gets even worse if we look at total expenditure or percent of GDP).

What can we learn from this? At face value, it seems as if California isn't spending educational dollars wisely, because a lot is being spent but students are still getting weak SERI scores.

However, the SERI score is not a measure of total "smartness" but only that related to math and science. So, maybe all those education dollars are simply not being allocated to math and science. If I were getting a grade on this, I would try to dig up that allocation data, but the 49ers vs. Saints game is on.

What do you think?
